CHAPTER 1


INTRODUCTION


1.1 Introduction Low-light image enhancement (LLIE) plays a crucial role in
improving the visual quality of images captured under sub-optimal illumination
conditions. It is an essential preliminary step for various downstream computer
vision applications, including autonomous driving, semantic segmentation, and
security surveillance. While human visual perception can adapt to varying
lighting, camera sensors often capture low-light images with severe noise, low
contrast, and degraded color fidelity. Conventional enhancement methods
primarily focus on uniform brightness adjustments but frequently overlook the
handling of non-uniform illumination. Consequently, these approaches often
result in overexposure in brighter regions, detail loss, and texture
degradation. To mitigate these limitations, this research proposes SwinLLIE
(Swin Transformer for Low-Light Image Enhancement), a novel hybrid architecture
that leverages multi-scale spatial feature learning to deliver improved
perceptual quality and structural integrity in enhanced low-light images.


1.2 Objectives The primary aim of this research is to design and develop a
highly efficient and accurate model for low-light image enhancement. To achieve
this, the following specific objectives have been defined:


  - Objective 1: To conduct a critical literature review of existing low-light
    image enhancement techniques, focusing on the transition from classical
    mathematical models to deep learning and modern Transformer-based
    architectures.
  - Objective 2: To design and develop a compact, hybrid CNN-Transformer
    architecture (SwinLLIE) that utilizes an encoder-decoder structure for
    multi-scale feature extraction while maintaining a low parameter count.
  - Objective 3: To formulate a novel, illumination-aware hybrid loss function
    that incorporates exposure control, color consistency, and edge preservation
    to mathematically prevent color shifting and overexposure artifacts.
  - Objective 4: To rigorously evaluate the proposed solution quantitatively and
    qualitatively across multiple benchmarks (LOL-v1 and LOL-v2 datasets) to
    validate cross-dataset generalization and computational efficiency.


1.3 Problem in Brief Enhancing low-light images captured in environments with
uneven lighting presents a significant challenge. Existing classical and early
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) methods typically apply global illumination
corrections, which inadvertently overexpose already bright areas and cause
significant color distortions. While recent Transformer-based methods achieve
high image fidelity, they often rely on massive network architectures with high
computational complexity (e.g., quadratic complexity in standard attention
mechanisms). This makes them unsuitable for deployment on resource-constrained
edge devices. There is a critical gap in achieving an optimal balance between
parameter efficiency, computational cost, and the perceptual quality of the
enhanced image.


1.4 Background and Motivation The motivation for this research stems from the
increasing reliance on computer vision systems in real-world, uncontrolled
environments. Systems such as nighttime autonomous driving or 24/7 security
surveillance require clear, high-fidelity visual inputs to function safely and
effectively. Historically, classical methods like histogram equalization and
Retinex theory provided foundational solutions but often resulted in unnatural
enhancements. The advent of deep learning brought CNNs and Generative
Adversarial Networks (GANs) into the domain, yet these too struggle with either
over-smoothing fine details or generating visual artifacts under complex
lighting. Recently, the Swin Transformer originally designed for image
classification has demonstrated exceptional capability in capturing both local
and global image dependencies through shifted window-based self-attention.
Adapting this powerful mechanism specifically for the unique illumination
challenges of low-light enhancement, without incurring prohibitive computational
costs, represents a highly motivated research opportunity.


1.5 Novel Approach to Low-Light Image Enhancement In contrast to conventional
techniques, this research introduces SwinLLIE, which utilizes a U-Net-based
generator integrated with Swin Transformer blocks for multi-scale feature
extraction. The architecture significantly diverges from standard adaptations by
being designed from the ground up for illumination handling. It incorporates a
window-based self-attention mechanism that reduces computational complexity from
O(N^2) to O(N \times w^2). Furthermore, a key novelty is the introduction of a
custom illumination-aware hybrid loss function. This function integrates five
complementary objectives: L1 reconstruction, VGG perceptual loss, color
consistency, edge preservation, and a custom exposure control loss that
explicitly suppresses highlights to prevent overexposure and color shifts.


1.6 Resource Requirements The development, training, and evaluation of the
SwinLLIE architecture require specific computational resources and software
environments:


  - Hardware: A single NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU with 16GB VRAM to handle the model
    training and multi-scale feature computations.
  - Software: Python programming language and the PyTorch 2.8 deep learning
    framework.
  - Data: The publicly available LOL (Low-Light) dataset benchmarks,
    specifically LOL-v1 for primary training/testing and the LOL-v2
    Real_captured dataset for cross-dataset generalization evaluation.

  - Chapter 6 (Implementation): Discusses the model setup, training parameters,

  - Chapter 7 (Evaluation): Presents the experimental setup, quantitative

  - Chapter 8 (Conclusions and Further Work): Summarizes the overall

1.8 Summary This chapter introduced the critical need for effective low-light

CHAPTER 2


LITERATURE REVIEW


2.1 Introduction Low-light image enhancement has been a long-standing challenge
in computer vision, evolving from simple mathematical pixel manipulations to
complex, data-driven deep learning architectures. This chapter critically
examines the historical progression and current state-of-the-art in low-light
image enhancement (LLIE). By exploring early classical methods, the shift
towards Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Generative Adversarial Networks
(GANs), and the recent dominance of Vision Transformers, this review highlights
the advancements and persistent limitations in the field. Ultimately, this
chapter identifies the research gaps that justify the development of the
proposed SwinLLIE architecture.


2.2 Chronological Review of Prior Work


2.2.1 Early development of low-light enhancement The earliest approaches to
low-light enhancement relied heavily on traditional image processing techniques.
Methods such as Histogram Equalization (HE) aimed to stretch the dynamic range
of an image, globally adjusting contrast. While computationally inexpensive, HE
typically amplifies background noise and causes severe color wash-out. A more
sophisticated mathematical foundation was established by the Retinex theory [3],
introduced by Land in 1977. Retinex theory posits that an image can be
decomposed into two components: illumination and reflectance. Early
Retinex-based algorithms attempted to estimate and remove the illumination map
to extract the clear reflectance map. Although these methods improved visibility
better than HE, they heavily relied on handcrafted priors and often produced
unnatural, over-enhanced results with significant color distortion.


2.2.2 Current developments and future trends The introduction of deep learning
marked a paradigm shift in LLIE. Early deep learning approaches utilized CNNs to
learn the mapping between low-light and normal-light images. For instance, LLNet
[4] employed a deep autoencoder to simultaneously denoise and enhance low-light
images. Subsequently, Retinex-Net integrated the classical Retinex theory with a
CNN framework, using sub-networks for decomposition and illumination adjustment.
Following CNNs, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) emerged as a powerful
tool for image restoration. EnlightenGAN[5] proposed an unsupervised generative
approach using a dual-discriminator setup, allowing the model to be trained
without paired datasets. Most recently, Vision Transformers (ViTs) have
disrupted the field. The Swin Transformer [1] introduced hierarchical feature
maps and shifted window-based attention, solving the quadratic computational
complexity of standard Transformers. This was quickly adapted for image
restoration tasks via SwinIR [2]. Current trends show a rapid movement towards
these attention-based mechanisms, with architectures like Retinexformer [12] and
SNR-Net [11] achieving state-of-the-art quantitative results.


2.2.3 Issues and research challenges Despite these advancements, several
critical challenges remain unresolved:


  - Non-Uniform Illumination: Many models apply uniform enhancement, which
    recovers dark areas but severely overexposes regions that already have
    adequate lighting.
  - Loss of Structural Integrity: CNNs often produce over-smoothed images,
    losing intricate textures and high-frequency details.
  - Computational Overhead: Recent high-performing Transformer models (e.g.,
    SNR-Net with 39.1M parameters) are too heavy for real-time edge deployment.
  - Evaluation Overfitting: A significant portion of existing literature
    exclusively benchmarks models on the LOL-v1 dataset, which only contains 15
    test images, failing to prove robust real-world generalization [27].


2.3 Comparative Analysis of Existing Technologies When comparing the three
primary deep learning paradigms for LLIE CNNs, GANs, and Transformers distinct
trade-offs emerge. CNN-based models (like LLNet) are relatively lightweight but
suffer from a limited receptive field, which restricts their ability to
understand global illumination contexts. Consequently, they often fail to
preserve sharp edges, leading to blurring. GAN-based models (like EnlightenGAN)
excel at perceptual enhancement and generating visually pleasing textures.
However, they are notoriously difficult to train, prone to mode collapse, and
often fabricate details that do not exist in the ground truth, lowering their
pixel-fidelity scores (such as PSNR). Transformer-based models (like LLFormer
[13] and Retinexformer [12]) overcome the receptive field limitations of CNNs by
using self-attention to capture global dependencies. They currently achieve the
highest PSNR and SSIM scores. However, the comparative drawback of standard
Transformers is their massive parameter count and computational footprint,
necessitating heavy GPU resources.


2.4 Strengths and Limitations of Current Technologies


  - Retinex-Net:
      - Strengths: Effectively decouples illumination from reflectance,
        providing a strong theoretical basis for enhancement.
      - Limitations: Often produces color shifts and halo artifacts around light
        sources due to imperfect decomposition.
  - EnlightenGAN [5]:
      - Strengths: Does not require paired low/normal light data; utilizes
        perceptual loss for highly realistic outputs.
      - Limitations: Struggles with intricate details in highly challenging
        lighting conditions; produces lower quantitative (PSNR) scores (18.23
        dB).
  - SNR-Net [11] & Retinexformer [12]:
      - Strengths: Achieve state-of-the-art signal-to-noise ratios and
        structural similarity (up to 25.16 dB PSNR).
      - Limitations: Highly inefficient. SNR-Net requires 39.1M parameters,
        making it unsuitable for mobile or embedded vision systems.
  - Swin-Unet adaptations [26]:
      - Strengths: Improves perceptual fidelity using LPIPS-based loss.
      - Limitations: Standard segmentation adaptations lack explicit highlight
        suppression, leaving them vulnerable to overexposure artifacts in
        mixed-lighting scenarios.


2.5 Summary of Issues A review of the literature reveals a persistent compromise
between enhancement quality and computational efficiency. Models that are
lightweight (CNNs) fail to handle complex illumination and texture preservation.
Conversely, models capable of high-fidelity restoration (recent Transformers)
carry immense computational costs. Furthermore, existing loss functions used in
these architectures generally optimize for global pixel accuracy but lack
specialized mechanisms to explicitly control highlights, resulting in persistent
color-shifting and overexposure in naturally bright sub-regions.


2.6 Discussion on gaps in current research The critical gap identified in
current research is the lack of a highly compact architecture specifically
designed from the ground up for illumination handling that mathematically
prevents overexposure. While the Swin Transformer has proven effective for
general image restoration (SwinIR [2]), its direct application to low-light
enhancement without architectural modifications for illumination variation is
suboptimal. Furthermore, the reliance on the small LOL-v1 test set limits the
proven reliability of many modern models.


2.6.1 Problem Definition Based on the identified gaps, the research problem is
defined as follows: How can a multi-scale Transformer-based architecture be
designed to effectively enhance low-light images with non-uniform illumination,
preventing overexposure and color distortion, while maintaining a sufficiently
compact parameter footprint for resource-constrained environments?


2.7 Summary This chapter systematically reviewed the evolution of low-light
image enhancement technologies. It highlighted the theoretical foundations of
early methods and critically analyzed the shift toward deep learning, detailing
the respective strengths and weaknesses of CNNs, GANs, and Transformers. The
review demonstrated that while Transformer models offer superior global context
understanding, they suffer from high computational complexity and struggle with
non-uniform highlight control. By identifying these gaps, this chapter
establishes the foundational necessity for the highly efficient, multi-scale,
and illumination-aware approach proposed in this research.


















CHAPTER 3


TECHNOLOGY ADOPTED


3.1 Introduction To successfully address the complexities of low-light image
enhancement, selecting the right computational frameworks and architectural
paradigms is critical. This chapter details the core technologies utilized in
the development of SwinLLIE. It outlines the rationale for moving away from
traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and standard Vision
Transformers in favor of a hybrid approach. Specifically, this chapter explores
the Swin Transformer architecture, the U-Net encoder-decoder structure, and the
software ecosystem (PyTorch) adopted to build, train, and validate the proposed
enhancement model.


3.2 Revisiting the research problem As defined in the previous chapters, the
primary research problem is the difficulty of enhancing images with non-uniform
illumination without causing overexposure, color distortion, or an unacceptable
loss of structural detail. Furthermore, while modern deep learning solutions
(such as standard Transformers) offer high perceptual fidelity by capturing
global image context, they introduce massive computational overhead. The
challenge, therefore, lies in engineering a solution that provides the global
context awareness of a Transformer and the localized detail preservation of a
CNN, all while strictly managing the parameter count to ensure the model remains
viable for deployment on resource-constrained hardware.


3.3 Justification for the choice of Technologies The selection of technologies
for SwinLLIE was driven by the dual requirements of high-quality image
reconstruction and computational efficiency:


  - Swin Transformer over Standard Vision Transformers (ViTs): Standard ViTs
    calculate self-attention across the entire image, resulting in a
    computational complexity that scales quadratically O(N^2) with the image
    resolution. This is prohibitive for high-resolution image restoration. The
    Swin Transformer was chosen because its window-based multi-head
    self-attention (W-MSA) restricts computation to non-overlapping local
    windows, reducing the complexity to linear scaling O(N \times w^2).
  - U-Net over Linear Architectures: Linear deep networks often struggle to
    preserve high-resolution spatial details as the image passes through
    successive layers. The U-Net architecture was chosen for its hierarchical
    encoder-decoder structure and skip connections, which allow high-frequency
    details from the input to bypass deep processing bottlenecks and directly
    inform the reconstruction phase.
  - PyTorch Deep Learning Framework: PyTorch (version 2.8) was selected over
    alternatives like TensorFlow due to its dynamic computational graph, which
    offers superior flexibility during the prototyping of complex, custom
    multi-component loss functions. Furthermore, PyTorch provides optimized
    parallel processing capabilities necessary for efficient training on an
    NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU.


3.4 Brief Overview of the Selected Technology


  - Swin Transformer: Originally introduced for image classification, the Swin
    (Shifted Window) Transformer builds hierarchical feature maps. It alternates
    between regular Window Multi-head Self-Attention (W-MSA) and Shifted Window
    Multi-head Self-Attention (SW-MSA). The W-MSA computes self-attention
    locally within a fixed grid, while the SW-MSA shifts the window partitioning
    in the subsequent layer to enable cross-window connections, allowing
    information to flow globally across the image.
  - U-Net Architecture: U-Net is a symmetric convolutional network design
    comprising a contracting path (encoder) to capture context and a symmetric
    expanding path (decoder) that enables precise localization. It relies
    heavily on skip connections that concatenate feature maps from the encoder
    directly to the corresponding layers in the decoder.
  - VGG-19 Network: The Visual Geometry Group (VGG) 19-layer network is a
    pre-trained image classification model. In this research, its pre-trained
    feature extraction layers are utilized not for classification, but to
    calculate the perceptual difference between the generated image and the
    ground truth.


3.5 Technology adoption for the solution In the SwinLLIE architecture, these
discrete technologies are fused into a cohesive pipeline. The U-Net serves as
the macro-architecture, providing the multi-scale spatial structure (shallow
feature extraction, deep bottleneck, and image reconstruction). However, the
standard convolutional blocks within the U-Net are replaced with Residual Swin
Transformer Blocks (RSTBs). By embedding Swin Transformer blocks inside the
U-Net, the model gains the ability to learn both local textures and global
illumination dependencies at three different structural scales. The PyTorch
framework is utilized to implement this hybrid architecture, manage the tensor
multiplications associated with the multi-head attention mechanisms, and compute
the backpropagation gradients during the training phase.


3.6 Novelty of the intended solution While both Swin Transformers and U-Nets are
established technologies, their specific integration and optimization in
SwinLLIE present several novelties tailored specifically for low-light
enhancement:


  - Illumination-Aware Hybrid Loss Function: The most significant novelty is the
    mathematical formulation of a custom 5-component loss function programmed
    into the PyTorch training loop. Unlike standard L1 or L2 loss functions,
    this hybrid function explicitly incorporates an exposure control loss with a
    defined highlight threshold (\tau_h = 0.95). This directly prevents the
    model from pushing pixel values into overexposure.
  - Learnable Skip-Connection Fusion: Standard U-Nets simply concatenate encoder
    and decoder features. SwinLLIE introduces a novel three-way feature fusion
    module (addition plus learned fusion via convolution) that intelligently
    weights how much low-level detail from the encoder should be preserved based
    on the specific illumination of the region.
  - Extreme Parameter Efficiency: By carefully tuning the embedding dimensions
    (60), encoder depths ([4,4,4]), and attention heads ([6,6,6]), the solution
    achieves state-of-the-art competitive structural similarity (0.840 SSIM)
    using only 4.68 million parameters an order of magnitude smaller than
    competing transformer models.


3.7 Summary This chapter detailed the technological foundations of the proposed
SwinLLIE framework. It justified the adoption of the Swin Transformer for its
computationally efficient self-attention mechanisms and the U-Net architecture
for its multi-scale feature preservation. Furthermore, it explained how these
technologies were synergistically combined within the PyTorch ecosystem to
create a highly efficient model. The chapter concluded by highlighting the
specific novelties of the solution, most notably the custom illumination-aware
loss function and the highly compact architectural footprint.












CHAPTER 4


APPROACH


4.1 Introduction This chapter outlines the conceptual methodology and overall
approach taken to solve the low-light image enhancement problem. While Chapter 3
discussed the underlying frameworks and Chapter 5 will detail the deep
architectural design, this chapter focuses on the high-level system workflow. It
defines the core hypothesis driving the research, clearly specifies the inputs
and outputs of the proposed system, maps out the sequential process workflow,
and identifies the target users who will ultimately benefit from the SwinLLIE
solution.


4.2 Hypothesis and its inspiration The inspiration for this approach stems from
the observation that existing low-light enhancement models typically fail in one
of two ways: they either apply uniform brightness adjustments that cause severe
overexposure in already-lit areas, or they utilize massive, computationally
heavy Transformer architectures that are impossible to deploy on edge devices.


From this observation, the core hypothesis of this research was formulated: It
is hypothesized that integrating window-based self-attention mechanisms (Swin
Transformers) into a multi-scale hierarchical framework (U-Net), guided by a
novel multi-objective loss function with explicit exposure control, will yield
high-fidelity image enhancement that prevents color shifts and overexposure,
while maintaining an ultra-compact parameter footprint compared to
state-of-the-art models.


4.3 Inputs and Outputs of the Solution The SwinLLIE system functions as an
image-to-image translation pipeline.


  - Inputs: The system accepts degraded, low-light RGB images characterized by
    heavy noise, low contrast, and obscured textures. During the training phase,
    these inputs are processed as random 96 \times 96 pixel patches extracted
    from high-resolution images to optimize computational memory. During the
    inference (testing/deployment) phase, the model accepts full-resolution
    low-light images.
  - Outputs: The final output is an enhanced RGB image of the same resolution as
    the input. The output image features natural, balanced illumination,
    restored color fidelity, suppressed noise, and preserved high-frequency
    structural details (such as edges and fine textures), without any
    overexposed highlight regions.


4.4 Process workflow in the solution The approach follows a systematic,
multi-stage workflow designed to progressively extract, enhance, and reconstruct
image features:


1.  Shallow Feature Extraction: The raw input image is first passed through a
    convolutional layer. This step maps the low-level pixel space into a
    higher-dimensional feature space, extracting basic visual elements like
    edges and color gradients.
2.  Multi-Scale Deep Feature Extraction (Encoder-Decoder): The shallow features
    are fed into a U-Net-based encoder-decoder structure.
      - Encoding: The image features are progressively downsampled. At each
        scale, Residual Swin Transformer Blocks (RSTBs) analyze the features to
        understand global illumination patterns.
      - Decoding: The features are progressively upsampled back to their
        original resolution, reconstructing the spatial dimensions.
3.  Learnable Feature Fusion: Throughout the encoder-decoder process, skip
    connections are utilized. A custom learnable fusion module intelligently
    combines the fine, high-resolution details from the encoder with the deep,
    context-rich illumination features from the decoder.
4.  Image Reconstruction: Finally, the fused deep features are passed through a
    final convolutional reconstruction layer that projects the feature maps back
    into standard RGB pixel space, generating the final enhanced image.
5.  Multi-Objective Loss Optimization (Training Only): During training, the
    output is compared against a ground-truth "normal light" image using
    the 5-component hybrid loss function to iteratively adjust the network's
    weights.


4.5 Justification for Technology adopted The multi-stage workflow adopted here
is justified by the dual nature of low-light degradation. Low-light images
suffer from macro-level issues (poor overall illumination) and micro-level
issues (noise and loss of fine texture). A standard linear network cannot
efficiently handle both simultaneously. By adopting a multi-scale approach, the
deep layers of the network can focus on global illumination correction, while
the shallow layers and skip connections ensure that micro-level textures are
preserved and reinjected into the final output. The adoption of the custom
hybrid loss function is justified as it provides multi-faceted gradient
feedback simultaneously penalizing blurriness (L1/VGG), color shifting
(Angular Color Loss), and blown-out highlights (Exposure Control Loss).


4.6 Target users The SwinLLIE architecture is designed to be highly
parameter-efficient (4.68M parameters), making it highly adaptable for various
target users and applications:


  - Autonomous Vehicle Manufacturers: Autonomous systems require clear,
    well-illuminated camera feeds for accurate object detection and semantic
    segmentation during nighttime driving.
  - Security and Surveillance Operators: CCTV systems often operate in
    sub-optimal lighting. SwinLLIE can be deployed on edge devices (like smart
    cameras) to enhance video frames in real-time.
  - Consumer Electronics Developers: Smartphone camera software requires
    lightweight algorithms for "Night Mode" photography that can run efficiently
    on mobile processors without draining battery life.
  - Computer Vision Researchers: As a baseline parameter-efficient model, other
    researchers can build upon SwinLLIE for related tasks like underwater image
    enhancement or medical image restoration.


4.7 Novelty and features of the solution The proposed approach introduces
several defining features that differentiate it from existing solutions:


  - Exposure-Controlled Enhancement: Unlike standard methods that blindly
    brighten the whole image, SwinLLIE features a mathematical thresholding
    mechanism that actively suppresses highlight regions, preventing the common
    "washed out" look in enhanced images.
  - Extreme Parameter Efficiency: The solution achieves competitive visual
    fidelity (0.840 SSIM) with a parameter footprint that is up to 8\times
    smaller than competing state-of-the-art Vision Transformer models.
  - Cross-Dataset Robustness: The approach is designed to generalize to unseen
    environments, explicitly moving away from overfitting to small, standardized
    testing sets.


4.8 Summary This chapter provided a conceptual overview of the SwinLLIE
methodology. It established the core hypothesis that combining Swin Transformers
with a multi-scale structure and a tailored loss function can yield
high-fidelity, computationally efficient low-light enhancement. The chapter
mapped out the end-to-end process workflow from shallow feature extraction to
final image reconstruction and identified the target users who will benefit from
this parameter-efficient solution. The subsequent chapter will delve into the
deep architectural analysis and mathematical design of these conceptual stages.

CHAPTER 5


ANALYSIS AND DESIGN


5.1 Introduction Building upon the conceptual methodology established in the
previous chapter, this chapter provides an in-depth technical analysis of the
SwinLLIE architecture. It details the internal mechanics, mathematical
formulations, and structural design of the machine learning model. By breaking
down the top-level architecture into its constituent modules including shallow
feature extraction, the multi-scale machine learning engine, and the feature
fusion modules this chapter explicitly illustrates how the model processes
low-light image tensors to achieve high-fidelity restoration.


5.2 Rationale for Design The architectural design of SwinLLIE is driven by the
necessity to capture both local textures and global illumination contexts
simultaneously. Standard CNNs possess a localized receptive field, which makes
them excellent at edge detection but poor at understanding large-scale
illumination gradients. Standard Vision Transformers (ViTs) calculate attention
globally, which is excellent for illumination but computationally prohibitive
for high-resolution images.


The rationale for the SwinLLIE design is to embed Swin Transformer blocks which
calculate self-attention within localized, shifting windows inside a U-Net
encoder-decoder hierarchy. This hybrid design allows the model to process
features at multiple scales, capturing global context at the deeper bottleneck
layers and fine spatial details at the shallower layers, drastically reducing
computational complexity while maintaining high structural similarity.


5.3 Top-Level Architecture The SwinLLIE network follows a U-shaped
encoder-decoder structure. The architecture consists of three main stages:
shallow feature extraction, deep feature extraction through encoder-decoder
processing, and image reconstruction.

5.3.1 Preprocessing Module (Shallow Feature Extraction) The initial phase of the
architecture prepares the raw image data for deep feature extraction. During
training, the input image is preprocessed by extracting overlapping 96 \times 96
pixel patches. These patches are passed through a shallow feature extraction
module consisting of a standard 3 \times 3 convolutional layer. This layer
projects the low-level RGB pixel space into a higher-dimensional feature space,
generating initial feature maps that preserve basic structural elements, which
are subsequently fed into the primary machine learning engine.


5.3.2 ML Engine (Deep Feature Extraction via RSTB) The core machine learning
engine of SwinLLIE relies on the Residual Swin Transformer Block (RSTB). Instead
of computing attention across the entire image (which has quadratic complexity),
the model utilizes a Window-based Multi-head Self-Attention (W-MSA) mechanism.
The feature map is partitioned into non-overlapping windows of size w = 8, and
attention is computed within each window:


Attention(Q, K, V) = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d}} + B\right)V \quad \quad (1)


Where Q, K, and V are the query, key, and value matrices respectively, d is the
dimension per head, and B is the relative position bias. For an image of size
H \times W, this windowed attention reduces computational complexity from
O((HW)^2) to O(HW \times w^2).


To enable cross-window connections and allow information to flow between
adjacent windows, the engine alternates standard W-MSA with Shifted Window
Multi-head Self-Attention (SW-MSA). Within each RSTB, a stack of these Swin
Transformer blocks is followed by a convolutional layer with a residual
connection:


\text{RSTB}(X) = \text{Conv}(\text{BasicLayer}(X)) + X \quad \quad (2)


The BasicLayer consists of the alternating W-MSA and SW-MSA blocks, defined
mathematically as:


X' = \text{W-MSA}(\text{LN}(X)) + X, \quad X'' = \text{MLP}(\text{LN}(X')) + X' \quad \quad (3)
Y' = \text{SW-MSA}(\text{LN}(X'')) + X'', \quad Y = \text{MLP}(\text{LN}(Y')) + Y' \quad \quad (4)


Where \text{LN} represents Layer Normalization, and \text{MLP} represents a
Multi-Layer Perceptron.


5.3.3 Extended module (Feature Fusion and Reconstruction) As the data passes
through the encoder, it is spatially downsampled to extract deep illumination
features. To reconstruct the image without losing high-resolution details, the
decoder progressively upsamples the features. SwinLLIE extends the standard
U-Net concatenation approach by introducing a learnable Feature Fusion module at
the skip connections.


To effectively combine the fine details from the encoder (F_{enc}) with the
context-rich features from the decoder (F_{dec}), the fusion module is
formulated as:


F_{fused} = F_{enc} + F_{dec} + \text{Conv}([F_{enc}, F_{dec}]) \quad \quad (5)


Where [ \cdot , \cdot ] denotes concatenation along the channel dimension. This
three-way combination (addition plus learned convolutional fusion) enables
highly effective multi-scale feature integration. Finally, the fused features
pass through a final convolution stage to reconstruct the enhanced RGB image.


5.4 Data Flow and Interaction Design The data flow in SwinLLIE follows a
distinct multi-scale pathway. The shallow features enter the encoder, which
consists of three hierarchical stages. As data flows downward through the
encoder, spatial dimensions are halved using patch merging, while the channel
dimensions are doubled (e.g., from 60 to 120, then to 240). At the lowest
resolution (the bottleneck), the deep RSTB extracts global illumination context.
The data then flows upward through the decoder, where spatial dimensions are
restored via patch expansion. At each upsampling step, the data interacts with
the corresponding spatial resolution from the encoder via the Feature Fusion
module, ensuring that the reconstructed image maintains crisp structural
boundaries.


5.5 Positioning the novelty within the Design The novelty of this design is
positioned at the intersection of architectural compactness and multi-scale
attention. While standard Swin-Unet architectures exist for medical image
segmentation, their direct application to low-light enhancement is sub-optimal.
SwinLLIE modifies this design paradigm by embedding the specialized 3-way
learnable feature fusion module (Equation 5) and operating with a highly
constrained embedding dimension (60) and MLP ratio (2.0). This specific
interaction design is what allows the architecture to achieve comprehensive
global and local feature extraction using only 4.68M parameters, significantly
positioning it ahead of heavier, less efficient state-of-the-art models.


5.6 Summary This chapter provided a detailed technical analysis of the SwinLLIE
model. The rationale for combining U-Net and Swin Transformers was established,
followed by a detailed breakdown of the three-stage architecture. The
mathematical formulations governing the Window-based Multi-head Self-Attention,
the Residual Swin Transformer Blocks, and the custom feature fusion mechanism
were thoroughly explained. Finally, the data flow through the multi-scale
hierarchy was mapped out, highlighting how the structural design contributes to
the model's novel parameter efficiency and high restoration capability.

IMPLEMENTATION


6.1 Introduction This chapter details the practical execution and implementation
of the SwinLLIE architecture. While the previous chapter focused on the
structural design and theoretical data flow, this chapter explains how that
design was translated into a functioning deep learning model. It outlines the
overall system development, the specific hardware and software environments
used, the module-wise implementation details, and the training hyperparameters.
Crucially, this chapter provides a comprehensive mathematical breakdown of the
algorithmic logic guiding the network: the custom 5-component illumination-aware
hybrid loss function.


6.2 Overall System Development The development of SwinLLIE followed a standard
deep learning pipeline. The process began with data preprocessing, where
high-resolution images from the LOL dataset were cropped into manageable
patches. Following this, the core SwinLLIE network was programmed using
object-oriented principles in Python, defining the encoder, decoder, and
attention blocks as customizable modules. The training loop was then implemented
to feed the image patches through the network (forward pass), compute the error
using the hybrid loss function, and update the network weights (backward pass)
iteratively over multiple epochs until convergence was achieved.


6.3 Hardware and Software Platforms To handle the computational demands of
multi-head self-attention and deep feature extraction, the system was
implemented using the following platforms:


  - Hardware: The model was trained and evaluated on a single NVIDIA Tesla T4
    GPU with 16GB of VRAM. This provided the necessary parallel processing power
    and memory capacity to train the network efficiently.
  - Software: The implementation was developed in Python. The core neural
    network architecture, tensor operations, and automatic differentiation were
    handled by the PyTorch 2.8 deep learning framework. CUDA was utilized to
    accelerate tensor operations on the GPU.


6.4 Module-wise Implementation of the design The SwinLLIE model was implemented
with a specific focus on parameter efficiency. The network was instantiated with
an embedding dimension of 60. The encoder and decoder depths were configured to
[4, 4, 4], and the number of attention heads at each respective scale was set to
[6, 6, 6]. The window size for the self-attention mechanism was strictly set
to 8, and the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) ratio was configured to 2.0. By
constraining these hyperparameters within the PyTorch modules, the total size of
the compiled model was kept to a highly compact 4.68 million parameters.


6.5 Algorithms and Pseudocode: The Hybrid Loss Function The core algorithmic
driver of the SwinLLIE implementation is the illumination-aware hybrid loss
function. This function guides the gradient descent process by balancing
reconstruction accuracy with perceptual quality. The total loss
(\mathcal{L}_{total}) is a weighted sum of five components:


\mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_1\mathcal{L}_{L1} + \lambda_v\mathcal{L}_{VGG} + \lambda_c\mathcal{L}_{color} + \lambda_e\mathcal{L}_{edge} + \lambda_x\mathcal{L}_{exp} \quad \quad (6)


Where the empirical weights are set to \lambda_1 = 1.0, \lambda_v = 0.1,
\lambda_c = 0.5, \lambda_e = 1.0, and \lambda_x = 1.0.


1) Reconstruction & Perceptual Loss: The primary reconstruction loss ensures
pixel-level accuracy using the L1 norm between the predicted image (I_{pred})
and the ground truth (I_{gt}):
\mathcal{L}_{L1} = \mathbb{E}[\|I_{pred} - I_{gt}\|_1] \quad \quad (7)


To preserve high-level features and textures, a perceptual loss is computed in a
pre-trained VGG19 feature space (\phi_i):
\mathcal{L}_{VGG} = \sum_i \|\phi_i(I_{pred}) - \phi_i(I_{gt})\|^2_2 \quad \quad (8)


2) Color & Edge Loss: To prevent the color shifts common in low-light
enhancement, an angular color consistency loss is implemented:
\mathcal{L}_{color} = 1 - \frac{1}{HW} \sum \frac{I_{pred} \cdot I_{gt}}{\|I_{pred}\|_2 \|I_{gt}\|_2 + \epsilon} \quad \quad (9)


Edge preservation is strictly enforced using Sobel operators E(\cdot) to
calculate the difference in high-frequency gradients:
\mathcal{L}_{edge} = \|E(I_{pred}) - E(I_{gt})\|_1 \quad \quad (10)


3) Exposure Control Loss: The most novel algorithmic component is the exposure
control loss, which prevents overexposure while preserving naturally bright
regions:
\mathcal{L}_{exp} = \|\text{ReLU}(I_{pred} - \tau_h)\|^2_2 + \lambda_b\|M_b \odot (I_{pred} - I_{gt})\|_1 \quad \quad (11)


Where \tau_h = 0.95 is the highlight threshold. Any predicted pixel
exceeding 0.95 is heavily penalized. M_b = \mathbb{1}[I_{gt} > 0.8] is a binary
mask identifying bright regions in the ground truth, \odot denotes element-wise
multiplication, and \lambda_b = 1.0 is the bright region penalty weight.


6.6 Workflow Diagrams / Flowcharts The execution workflow during the training
phase operates in a continuous loop. An input batch of low-light patches is
passed through the SwinLLIE forward method. The output tensor is then passed
into the custom Loss Module alongside the ground-truth tensor. The Loss Module
independently calculates the L1, VGG, Color, Edge, and Exposure losses, sums
them according to their predefined weights, and returns a single scalar loss
value. PyTorch's backward() function is then called on this scalar to compute
the gradients across the network, after which the optimizer steps forward to
update the weights.


6.7 Integration of Components Integrating the model architecture with the loss
function required a carefully tuned optimization strategy. The AdamW optimizer
was utilized with parameters set to \beta_1 = 0.9, \beta_2 = 0.999, and a weight
decay of 10^{-4}. To ensure stable convergence, a cosine annealing learning rate
schedule was integrated. The initial learning rate was set to 2 \times 10^{-4},
decaying to a minimum of 10^{-6}, with a 5-epoch warmup phase. During training,
random 96 \times 96 patches were utilized with a batch size of 8. To prevent
exploding gradients during the complex loss calculations, gradient clipping was
enforced at a value of 1.0.


6.8 Incremental Testing During Development To monitor the model's learning
progress and prevent overfitting, incremental testing was integrated into the
development cycle. The model was trained for a total of 100 epochs, which took
approximately 34 minutes on the Tesla T4 GPU. At the end of each epoch, the
model was evaluated against a validation set. The Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio
(PSNR) was calculated for the validation outputs. Instead of simply saving the
model at the final epoch, an incremental checkpointing system was implemented
where the model weights were only saved if the current epoch's validation PSNR
exceeded the previous best. This ensured that the highest-performing model
configuration was retained for final evaluation.


6.9 Summary This chapter provided a comprehensive overview of the SwinLLIE
implementation process. It detailed the specific PyTorch configurations, the
NVIDIA hardware utilized, and the exact hyperparameters (depths, heads, learning
rates, optimizer settings) required to reproduce the 4.68M parameter model. Most
importantly, it provided a rigorous mathematical breakdown of the 5-component
hybrid loss function, explaining how elements like the exposure control loss and
angular color consistency are algorithmically enforced to prevent overexposure
and color shifting during the training phase.

8.1 Introduction This final chapter concludes the research undertaken to develop

8.2 Overall Conclusion This research successfully proposed, developed, and

image enhancement. By embedding Window-based Multi-head Self-Attention
mechanisms within a multi-scale U-Net encoder-decoder structure, the model
effectively balanced the extraction of global illumination context with the
preservation of high-frequency local textures. Crucially, the implementation of
a custom 5-component hybrid loss function mathematically resolved persistent
issues in the domain, specifically the occurrence of color shifts and the
overexposure of naturally bright sub-regions. Ultimately, SwinLLIE proved that
it is possible to achieve highly competitive perceptual image restoration while
maintaining a compact parameter footprint, making it a viable bridge between
lightweight legacy CNNs and prohibitively heavy modern Vision Transformers.


8.3 Objectives-wise Achievements The success of this research is demonstrated
through the fulfillment of the specific objectives established at the outset of
the study:


  - Objective 1 (Literature Review): Achieved. A critical review of classical
    methods, CNNs, GANs, and Transformers was conducted, successfully
    identifying the dual problem of parameter inefficiency in modern ViTs and
    the lack of explicit highlight suppression in standard loss functions.
  - Objective 2 (Architecture Design): Achieved. The SwinLLIE model was
    successfully designed and implemented in PyTorch, utilizing Residual Swin
    Transformer Blocks (RSTBs) and a learnable feature fusion module to maintain
    an ultra-compact footprint of just 4.68 million parameters.
  - Objective 3 (Loss Function Formulation): Achieved. An illumination-aware
    hybrid loss function was successfully formulated, integrating L1, VGG
    perceptual, angular color consistency, edge preservation, and a custom
    exposure control loss (\tau_h = 0.95) that actively suppressed overexposure
    artifacts.
  - Objective 4 (Cross-Dataset Evaluation): Achieved. The solution was
    rigorously evaluated on both the LOL-v1 (eval15) and LOL-v2 (Real_captured)
    datasets, successfully validating the model's high perceptual quality and
    its robust generalization to 100 completely unseen real-world image pairs.

highly competitive with state-of-the-art models like SNR-Net (0.842 SSIM),

test set.


8.5 Challenges and Limitations Despite its strong structural similarity and
parameter efficiency, the SwinLLIE architecture has specific limitations.


  - Absolute PSNR Gap: While SwinLLIE achieves competitive SSIM, there remains a
    noticeable gap in absolute PSNR when compared to recent, significantly
    heavier transformer architectures (e.g., Retinexformer achieves 25.16 dB
    compared to SwinLLIE's 19.09 dB). This gap is a direct trade-off
    attributable to the tightly constrained model capacity (4.68M parameters).
  - Computational Inference Cost: Although the parameter count is small, the
    model still requires 267.67 GFLOPs for inference at a standard
    400 \times 600 resolution. While this is a massive improvement over standard
    global attention Transformers, this computational overhead may still limit
    strictly real-time video processing (e.g., 60 frames per second) on
    extremely low-end, battery-powered edge devices without further
    optimization.


8.6 Future Work To address the identified limitations and expand the
applicability of this research, several directions for future work are proposed:


  - Model Pruning and Knowledge Distillation: To further optimize the
    efficiency-quality trade-off and reduce the 267.67 GFLOP requirement, future
    research will explore knowledge distillation techniques (training a smaller
    SwinLLIE student model from a massive teacher model) and network pruning to
    enable true real-time inference.
  - Unsupervised Training Paradigms: Currently, SwinLLIE relies on paired
    datasets (low-light and corresponding normal-light ground truths). Future
    work will explore adapting the architecture and hybrid loss function for
    unsupervised training paradigms, removing the reliance on
    difficult-to-acquire paired data.
  - Extension to Video Enhancement: The multi-scale windowed attention mechanism
    can be extended temporally. Future iterations of SwinLLIE could
    incorporate 3D convolutions or temporal attention windows to ensure smooth,
    flicker-free enhancement across consecutive frames in low-light video feeds.
